Introduction to Protein Sorting

Protein sorting (or protein targeting) is the biological mechanism by which proteins are transported to their appropriate destinations within or outside the cell. Proteins can be targeted to the inner space of an organelle, an intracellular membrane, the plasma membrane, or to exterior of the cell via secretion.

Signal Peptides (Sorting Signals)

The "zip codes" of the cell. These are specific amino acid sequences that determine the destination of a protein.

  • Signal Sequence: A continuous stretch of 15-60 amino acids, typically at the N-terminus. Often cleaved off by a signal peptidaseAn enzyme that cleaves the signal peptide once the protein reaches its destination. after reaching the destination.
  • Signal Patch: Formed by 3D folding of the protein bringing distantly separated amino acids together. Typically found on proteins destined for the nucleus or lysosomes.
Key Concept: If you attach an ER signal sequence to a cytosolic protein via genetic engineering, that protein will be redirected to the ER. This proves signal sequences are both necessary and sufficient for targeting.

Protein Translocation Mechanism (ER)

The entry point for the secretory pathway is the Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER). This utilizes the Signal Recognition Particle (SRP).


  1. Translation begins on a free ribosome in the cytosol.
  2. The N-terminal hydrophobic signal sequence emerges from the ribosome.
  3. SRP binds to the signal sequence and the ribosome, temporarily pausing translation.
  4. The SRP-ribosome complex binds to the SRP receptor on the ER membrane.
  5. The ribosome is transferred to the Translocon (Sec61 complex), a protein-lined channel.
  6. SRP is released (requires GTP hydrolysis), translation resumes, and the growing polypeptide is threaded into the ER lumen.
  7. Signal peptidase cleaves the signal sequence.

Protein Sorting to Organelles

Proteins destined for the nucleus contain a Nuclear Localization Signal (NLS), usually rich in basic amino acids like Lysine and Arginine (e.g., PKKKRKV). They are transported folded through the Nuclear Pore Complex (NPC).

  • Importin binds the NLS and escorts the cargo through the NPC.
  • Inside the nucleus, Ran-GTP binds to Importin, causing it to release the cargo.
  • The Importin-Ran-GTP complex exits to the cytosol, where GTP is hydrolyzed to GDP.

Proteins possess an N-terminal targeting sequence that forms an amphipathic alpha-helix (positively charged on one side, hydrophobic on the other).

  • Proteins are kept unfolded in the cytosol by Hsp70 chaperones.
  • They enter via the TOM complex (Translocase of the Outer Membrane).
  • They cross the inner membrane via the TIM complex (Translocase of the Inner Membrane), driven by membrane potential and mitochondrial Hsp70.

Similar to mitochondria but uses a Transit Peptide (rich in Ser, Thr, small hydrophobic AAs). Translocons used are TOC (Outer) and TIC (Inner).

Proteins contain a Peroxisomal Targeting Signal (PTS). The most common is PTS1, a C-terminal SKL (Serine-Lysine-Leucine) motif. Proteins are transported in their fully folded state.

Lysosomal hydrolases receive a specific sugar tag in the cis-Golgi network: Mannose-6-Phosphate (M6P). M6P receptors in the trans-Golgi bind these proteins and package them into Clathrin-coated vesicles bound for the endosome/lysosome.

Vesicular Transport & Secretory Pathway

Once inside the ER, proteins travel to the Golgi and beyond via transport vesicles. Vesicle budding is driven by specific coat proteins.

Clathrin Trans-Golgi → Lysosomes/Endosomes/Plasma Membrane ARF
Mechanism of Fusion: Vesicle targeting and fusion to the correct target membrane is mediated by Rab GTPases (for tethering) and SNARE proteins. v-SNAREs (vesicle) bind to t-SNAREs (target), winding together tightly to force membrane fusion.

Diseases Related to Defective Protein Sorting

Mistargeting of proteins leads to severe pathologies:

  • I-cell Disease (Mucolipidosis II): Defect in the enzyme that adds the Mannose-6-Phosphate tag. Lysosomal enzymes are secreted into the blood instead of going to the lysosome, leading to cellular waste accumulation.
  • Cystic Fibrosis: A mutation (often ΔF508) in the CFTR protein causes slight misfolding. The ER quality control mechanism flags it as defective and destroys it, preventing it from reaching the plasma membrane.
  • Zellweger Syndrome: Mutations in peroxin (PEX) genes lead to empty peroxisomes because proteins fail to be imported. Causes severe neurological defects.

Quick Revision Before Exam

  • KDEL signal: ER retention signal (returns proteins from Golgi to ER via COPI).
  • M6P: Lysosome targeting signal.
  • NLS: Nuclear targeting (rich in basic amino acids like Lys, Arg).
  • PTS1 (SKL): Peroxisome targeting.
  • TOM/TIM: Mitochondria. TOC/TIC: Chloroplast.
  • SNAREs: Mediate vesicle fusion.
  • Ran-GTPase: Drives nuclear transport directionality.

Frequently Asked Questions (MSc Level)

The TOM/TIM translocation channels are narrow. If a protein folds in the cytosol, it cannot pass through. Cytosolic chaperones of the Hsp70 family bind to the nascent polypeptide, utilizing ATP to prevent premature folding until it enters the mitochondrial matrix.

Once the N-terminal signal sequence enters the lumen of the ER (or mitochondria/chloroplast), a membrane-bound enzyme called Signal Peptidase recognizes a specific cleavage site adjacent to the signal sequence and hydrolyzes the peptide bond, releasing the mature protein.

The asymmetric distribution of the Ran-GTPase system. High Ran-GTP in the nucleus (maintained by Ran-GEF attached to chromatin) and high Ran-GDP in the cytosol (maintained by Ran-GAP on cytosolic fibrils) dictate that cargo loading/unloading only happens in the correct compartment.
